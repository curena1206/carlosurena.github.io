Payments Franchise Economics

Payments portfolios are typically managed as products. That is where margin disappears. The difference typically appears in four places:

Pricing architecture and exception governance
Corridor-level profitability visibility
Infrastructure decisions tied to commercial return
Portfolio-level capital allocation

When those systems align, payments stops behaving like a utility and starts behaving like a franchise business.

Operator Proof

At Citi, Deutsche Bank, HSBC, and Mashreq, I built pricing governance and commercialization frameworks for wire and cross-border products. This work consistently focused on one outcome: restoring margin where it was being silently lost.

This included:

  • Corridor pricing models and exception governance
  • Portfolio margin monitoring across multi-rail platforms
  • Commercialization strategies connecting payment flows to balance sheet value
  • Go-to-market frameworks for ACH, wires, RTP, and cross-border products
The work is straightforward in principle: find where value is being left on the table, build the system to capture it, and make sure it holds.
How I Think About Payments P&L

Most payments franchises are flying blind on their own economics. Revenue is growing, transaction volume is rising, and leadership assumes the business is healthy. It usually isn't. Pricing leakage, operational drag, exception costs, and governance gaps quietly compress margin — often for years before anyone notices.

Margin compression often sits in the 10%-30% range once full cost is applied. When I walk into an underperforming payments franchise, I don't start with the sales pipeline. I start with the operating model:

  • Pricing structure
  • Governance framework
  • KPI discipline
  • Cost-to-serve visibility

That's where the real leverage is.
