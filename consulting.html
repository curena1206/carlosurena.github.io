The Problem

This is where the gap between revenue and margin becomes visible.

Most payment portfolios are managed by product. Their economics are managed by nobody.

Banks organize payments with separate product ownership across ACH, wires, cross-border, and FX. Each team optimizes its own product. Payments portfolio economics across rails and corridors rarely has a single owner.

The same dynamics apply to networks and fintech platforms building multi-rail businesses at scale.

In most multi-rail portfolios, fewer than 25% of payment flows generate the bulk of the margin. The rest dilute it through pricing exceptions, routing inefficiencies, and operational cost structures that remain invisible at the product level, often for years before anyone notices.

This is where margin is lost quietly. Not because revenue is absent, but because its economics are never fully seen.

01

Pricing Leakage

Override rates accumulate without review. Exceptions never expire. Pricing decisions are made at the relationship level with no visibility into true corridor margin. Revenue looks healthy. Net margin tells a different story.

02

Payment Economics Lack Visibility

Banks track volumes and revenues. They rarely track network and external processing costs, funding and liquidity costs, compliance overhead, and operational exceptions at the flow level. Flows that look profitable on revenue are often margin-negative once full costs are applied.

03

Infrastructure Disconnected From Economics

Rail decisions are made historically. A client asked, a product team responded, an integration became a dependency. Nobody evaluated the routing economics. The result is a cost structure nobody chose and few can change.

Why Now
